1
Introduction

Who Is Jesus?

You've probably heard the name Jesus before. Maybe at church. Maybe in a prayer. Maybe your family talks about him sometimes. But do you know who he actually is?

Here's something amazing: Jesus isn't just someone who lived a long time ago in a book. He's real. And according to Christians, he's alive right now — and he knows your name.

"For God so loved the world that he gave his only Son, so that everyone who believes in him may not perish but may have eternal life."
John 3:16 (NRSV)

That verse is one of the most famous sentences ever written. It says that God loves the world — and that means you. This whole study is about understanding that love better by getting to know Jesus: who he was, what he did, and why it matters today.

Key Word
Gospel

The word "Gospel" means Good News. The four Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — are books in the Bible that tell the story of Jesus's life. In this study, we'll read from Matthew, Luke, and John.

In this study, you'll follow Jesus from the night he was born in a stable to the morning he rose from the dead. Along the way, you'll see what he taught, how he helped people, and why billions of people across history have believed he changed everything.

Think About It

Before we start — what do you already know about Jesus? And what's one thing you're curious about?

2
The Birth

God Enters the World in Bethlehem

Imagine the most powerful being in the universe deciding to become a tiny baby — helpless, crying, needing to be held. That's exactly what Christians believe happened on the first Christmas.

Jesus was born in Bethlehem, a small town in Israel, about 2,000 years ago. His mother was Mary, a young woman from a town called Nazareth. His father on earth was Joseph, a carpenter. But Christians believe Jesus also had a heavenly Father — God himself.

Luke 2:6–7
"While they were there, the time came for her to give birth. And she gave birth to her firstborn son and wrapped him in bands of cloth, and laid him in a manger, because there was no place for them in the inn."
A manger is a feeding trough for animals — like a wooden box full of hay. Jesus was born in a stable, surrounded by animals, not in a palace. That tells us something important right away about who he came for.

Shepherds were the first people to hear the news. In those days, shepherds were considered low-class — not important people. But an angel appeared to them first, not to kings or priests. Later, wise men from far away followed a star to find the baby. They brought expensive gifts. Rich and poor, near and far: Jesus came for everyone.

God didn't arrive in a palace. He arrived in a stable — so that no one would ever feel too poor or too small to find him.

Key Word
Incarnation

This is the big word for what happened at Christmas. "Incarnation" means "God becoming flesh" — God becoming a real human being. Christians believe Jesus is both fully God and fully human at the same time. It's one of the most amazing ideas in history.

Think About It

Why do you think God chose to be born as a baby instead of appearing as something more powerful and impressive? What does that tell you about God?

Talk About It
  • The shepherds were the first to hear — not kings or priests. Why might that be?
  • If God was going to be born as a human, what would you have chosen for him to be born into — a palace, a regular house, or a stable? Why?

3
Growing Up

The Hidden Life in Nazareth

Here's something surprising: we know almost nothing about the first 30 years of Jesus's life. The Bible tells us he was born, and then — almost nothing — until he was an adult. Jesus spent most of his life as an ordinary person.

He grew up in Nazareth, a small, not-very-important town. He learned carpentry from Joseph. He ate meals with his family. He went to synagogue on the Sabbath. He was a kid — just like you.

Luke 2:52
"And Jesus increased in wisdom and in years, and in divine and human favor."
Jesus grew up. He learned things. He got taller and smarter over time — just like every human being does. This matters: Jesus knows what it's like to be young, to learn, to not know everything yet.

There is one story from his childhood: when Jesus was 12, his family traveled to Jerusalem for a big religious festival called Passover. On the way home, Mary and Joseph couldn't find him. They searched for three days — three terrifying days for any parent — and finally found him in the Temple, talking with the religious teachers.

Luke 2:49
"He said to them, 'Why were you searching for me? Did you not know that I must be in my Father's house?'"
Even at 12, Jesus knew who his Father really was. But notice: after this, he went home with Mary and Joseph and was obedient to them. He honored his parents, even knowing he was the Son of God.
Why It Matters
Jesus Was Really Human

Jesus didn't just pretend to be human. He really grew up. He really learned things. He knows what it's like to have homework, to feel hungry, to get tired, to miss people. He's not far away from your life — he lived one.

Think About It

Have you ever had a moment where you felt really sure about something — like you knew exactly what you were supposed to do? Jesus had that at 12. What about you?

4
His Ministry Begins

Baptism, Temptation, and the First Disciples

When Jesus was about 30 years old, something changed. He left Nazareth and began his public life — the three years that would change the world forever.

It started at the Jordan River, where a man named John the Baptist was calling people to repent and be baptized. Jesus came to be baptized — not because he needed forgiveness (Christians believe he never sinned), but to show that he was on our side, standing with us in our need.

"And a voice came from heaven: 'You are my Son, the Beloved; with you I am well pleased.'"
Luke 3:22 (NRSV)

Right after his baptism, Jesus went alone into the desert for 40 days. He didn't eat. He prayed. And there, the devil came and tried to tempt him three times — offering him food, power, and fame. Jesus refused every time, using Scripture to respond.

Why It Matters
Jesus Was Tempted Too

Jesus knows what it's like to be tempted — to be pressured to do something wrong. He didn't give in. And because he went through it, he understands when you're facing something hard.

After the desert, Jesus began calling people to follow him. He walked along the Sea of Galilee and saw two brothers — Peter and Andrew — fishing. He said simply: "Follow me." They dropped their nets and went. He said the same to James and John. Same response. These were the first disciples: ordinary working people, not religious scholars or wealthy men.

Talk About It
  • Why do you think Jesus chose fishermen instead of priests or rulers to be his first followers?
  • If Jesus walked up to you and said "Follow me" — what do you think that would feel like?
  • Have you ever been tempted to do something wrong? What helped you say no?
Think About It

Peter, Andrew, James, and John left their jobs immediately to follow Jesus. What do you think made them do that? What would make you trust someone that much?

5
His Miracles

What the Miracles Reveal About Who He Is

During his three years of ministry, Jesus did things no one had ever seen before. He healed people who were sick. He made blind people see. He fed thousands of people with just five loaves of bread and two fish. He walked on water. He even raised people from the dead.

But here's the thing — the miracles aren't just impressive tricks. Each one says something about who Jesus is and what he cares about.

John 6:10–11
"Jesus said, 'Make the people sit down.' ... Jesus then took the loaves, and when he had given thanks, he distributed them to those who were seated; so also the fish, as much as they wanted."
Five thousand people were hungry. Jesus fed them all — with a little boy's lunch. The miracle says: Jesus sees people who are in need, and he has more than enough to give.
  • Healing the sick — Jesus saw people others ignored. He touched people nobody wanted to touch. He cared about suffering bodies, not just souls.
  • Calming a storm — The disciples were terrified. Jesus spoke to the storm and it stopped. He has authority over even the things that scare us most.
  • Raising Lazarus — Jesus arrived after his friend Lazarus had died. He wept. Then he called Lazarus out of the tomb. Even death isn't bigger than Jesus.

Jesus didn't do miracles to show off. He did them because he saw real people with real pain — and he couldn't walk past without helping.

John 11:35
"Jesus wept."
The shortest verse in the Bible. When Lazarus died, Jesus cried — even though he was about to raise him from the dead. He felt the sadness of the people who loved Lazarus. Jesus isn't distant from our grief. He enters it with us.
Think About It

Have you ever felt afraid or sad and wished someone would just be there with you? Jesus wept with his friends. How does it feel to know he enters our pain like that?

6
His Teaching

The Beatitudes and the Two Great Commandments

Jesus was an extraordinary teacher. People came from all over to hear him — thousands of them, sitting on hillsides and lakeshores, hanging on every word. His teaching turned a lot of ideas upside down.

One of his most famous speeches is called the Sermon on the Mount. It begins with the Beatitudes — a list of surprising blessings.

Key Word
Beatitude

"Beatitude" comes from a Latin word meaning happiness or blessing. But Jesus's idea of who is blessed is completely different from what the world usually says.

Matthew 5:3–6
"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled."
The world says: blessed are the powerful, the popular, the rich. Jesus says: blessed are those who feel small, those who are sad, those who are kind. He's describing a completely different way of measuring a good life.

When someone asked Jesus what the most important commandment was, he didn't recite a long list. He said two things:

"Love the Lord your God with all your heart, soul, and mind. And love your neighbor as yourself." — Matthew 22:37–39

That's it. Everything else — all 613 commandments in Jewish law — hangs on those two. Love God, love people. Jesus said if you get those two things right, everything else follows.

Talk About It
  • Who is the most "meek" (gentle and humble) person you know? What is it like to be around them?
  • Who counts as your "neighbor" according to Jesus? Is it just people who live next door?
  • What would school look like if everyone followed the two great commandments?
Think About It

Which Beatitude feels most surprising to you — which one do you not expect God to say is "blessed"? Why?

7
The Cross

Why Jesus Died

This is the hardest part of the story. After three years of teaching and healing and calling people to follow him, Jesus was arrested, put on trial, and executed on a cross. His disciples ran away. His friends were devastated. It seemed like everything was over.

Why did this happen? The religious leaders felt threatened by him. The Roman governor, Pilate, gave in to pressure even though he thought Jesus was innocent. And Jesus himself didn't fight back — even though he could have.

Luke 23:34
"Jesus said, 'Father, forgive them; for they do not know what they are doing.'"
Even while being crucified, Jesus prayed for the people doing it to him. That's not how people usually respond to being hurt. Christians believe this is what God's love actually looks like — it doesn't stop, not even in the worst moments.

Christians believe Jesus's death wasn't a mistake or a defeat. They believe he died to carry something for us — all the ways we've turned away from God, all the wrong things we've done, all the distance between us and him. The Cross is the place where that distance was closed forever.

Key Word
Sacrifice

A sacrifice is when you give something precious so someone else doesn't have to suffer. Christians believe Jesus gave his life as a sacrifice — not because God was angry and needed to punish someone, but because love sometimes goes all the way to the end for the person it loves.

Jesus didn't die because he had to. He died because he chose to — because he loved us that much.

Think About It

Have you ever done something hard or uncomfortable because you loved someone and wanted to help them? That's a tiny glimpse of what Jesus did. How does it feel to think about someone doing that for you?

8
The Resurrection

The Empty Tomb — and What It Means for Us

Three days after Jesus died and was buried, some of his followers went to the tomb early on Sunday morning. They expected to find a body. Instead, the tomb was empty. The stone had been rolled away. And then — they saw Jesus. Alive.

"He is not here; for he has been raised, as he said. Come, see the place where he lay."
Matthew 28:6 (NRSV)

The disciples were terrified at first. Then overwhelmed. Then filled with a joy they couldn't explain. Over the next 40 days, Jesus appeared to hundreds of people — eating with them, talking with them, showing them his wounds so they'd know it was really him.

Christians call this the Resurrection — and they believe it's the most important event in all of history. Why? Because if death couldn't hold Jesus, then death doesn't have the final word for anyone who belongs to him.

Key Word
Resurrection

Resurrection means coming back to life — not like a ghost, but with a real body. Jesus's resurrection wasn't like a near-death experience or a spiritual vision. Christians believe he was physically raised from the dead, and that this is the guarantee of our own future resurrection.

And here's what makes it personal: the first person Jesus spoke to after the resurrection was Mary Magdalene — alone, weeping in a garden, thinking everything was lost. He called her by name. Just her name. And everything changed.

Jesus called her by name. He knows your name too. That's what the whole story has been pointing toward.

  • The empty tomb means — death doesn't win. Love does.
  • The resurrection means — Jesus is alive right now, not just in history.
  • For you it means — the person who died for you is also the person who is present with you today.
Talk About It
  • Why do you think Jesus called Mary Magdalene by name instead of just appearing to everyone at once?
  • If the resurrection really happened, what difference should it make for how we live today?
  • What's the most important thing you've learned about Jesus in this whole study?
Final Reflection

At the beginning we asked: who is Jesus? Now that you've followed his whole story — what would you say? Who is he to you?
