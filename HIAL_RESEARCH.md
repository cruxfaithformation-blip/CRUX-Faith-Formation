# Here I Am, Lord — Research & Scripture Reference

Chapters 3–12 in order. Read these passages on the plane. Each entry includes the key texts, story summary, Turning Point, Here I Am angle, hero trait, and memory verse candidate.

---

## Part One: When God Calls

### Chapter 3 — Joseph
**Hero Trait:** Unbreakable Faithfulness

**Read:** Genesis 37, 39–50
*(The whole Joseph arc. It's long but worth it — one of the most complete stories in the Old Testament.)*

**Story in brief:** Seventeen-year-old Joseph is his father Jacob's favorite son. His brothers are so jealous they sell him to slave traders headed to Egypt. He ends up in Potiphar's house, gets falsely accused by Potiphar's wife, lands in prison, interprets dreams, gets forgotten, waits, and eventually rises to become second-in-command of all Egypt — just in time to save his family (and his brothers) from famine.

**The Turning Point:** When Joseph finally reveals himself to his brothers, they are terrified. He could destroy them. Instead he says: *"You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good."* He spent decades in a hole — and came out without bitterness.

**Here I Am angle:** When someone hurts you badly or treats you unfairly, you still get to choose who you become. Joseph never let his circumstances determine his character. He kept working, kept trusting, kept showing up — in the pit, in the prison, and in the palace.

**Memory verse:** Genesis 50:20 — *"You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good, to accomplish what is now being done, the saving of many lives."*

**Notes for writing:**
- The coat of many colors (Genesis 37:3) is the detail every kid knows — use it in the hook
- Joseph interprets dreams twice: for the cupbearer and baker in prison (Genesis 40), and for Pharaoh (Genesis 41)
- He hides his identity from his brothers across multiple visits before the reveal (Genesis 42–45) — great dramatic tension
- He weeps more than any other character in Genesis

## Part Two: When the Stakes Are Highest

### Chapter 4 — Daniel
**Hero Trait:** Uncompromising Faith

**Read:** Daniel 1, 3, 6
*(Chapter 1: Daniel refuses the king's food. Chapter 3: Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego in the fiery furnace — Daniel's friends, not Daniel himself, but essential context. Chapter 6: the lion's den.)*

**Story in brief:** Daniel is taken from Jerusalem to Babylon as a teenager when King Nebuchadnezzar conquers Israel. He's selected for royal training but refuses to eat the king's food because it violates his faith. He asks to eat only vegetables — and ends up healthier than everyone else. He goes on to interpret the king's dreams and rise in the courts of multiple kings. When a law is passed making prayer illegal, he prays anyway. He's thrown to the lions. He walks out unharmed.

**The Turning Point:** Daniel 6:10 — When Daniel hears the new law banning prayer, he doesn't protest, doesn't hide, doesn't quit. He goes to his window, opens it toward Jerusalem, and prays exactly as he always has. Three times a day. In plain sight.

**Here I Am angle:** When following the rules means hiding your faith — at school, online, with friends who think it's weird — Daniel kept going. Not loudly. Not to make a point. Just quietly, faithfully, the same as always.

**Memory verse:** Daniel 6:10 — *"He went home to his upstairs room where the windows opened toward Jerusalem. Three times a day he got down on his knees and prayed, giving thanks to his God, just as he had done before."*

**Notes for writing:**
- The fiery furnace (Daniel 3) is Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego — not Daniel — but the line "even if he does not, we will not serve your gods" (3:18) is one of the greatest lines in scripture. Worth referencing.
- God is not absent from Daniel — He's working in every scene, even when it looks like Daniel is losing
- Daniel is a teenager when this starts — important to establish for young readers

### Chapter 5 — Ruth
**Hero Trait:** Loyal Love

**Read:** Ruth 1–4
*(The whole book. It's only four chapters and reads like a short story.)*

**Story in brief:** Naomi moves to Moab with her husband and two sons. The husband dies. Both sons marry Moabite women — Ruth and Orpah. Both sons then die. Naomi has nothing left. She tells her daughters-in-law to go home to their own families. Orpah goes. Ruth refuses. She follows Naomi back to Israel, works in the fields of a man named Boaz to gather leftover grain, and eventually Boaz — a relative of Naomi's — marries her and redeems the family line. Ruth becomes the great-grandmother of King David.

**The Turning Point:** Ruth 1:16–17 — *"Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God."* The easier path was going home. She chose the harder, costly one instead.

**Here I Am angle:** There are moments when walking away would be completely understandable — from a friend who's struggling, a family member who needs you, a commitment that got hard. Ruth stayed anyway. Not because it was easy. Because it was right.

**Memory verse:** Ruth 1:16 — *"Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God."*

**Notes for writing:**
- Ruth is a foreigner — a Moabite in Israel. She has no reason to stay, no claim on anything. That's what makes her loyalty so striking.
- Boaz notices Ruth because of her reputation: *"All the people of my town know that you are a woman of noble character"* (Ruth 3:11) — her faithfulness is visible
- Ruth appears in Jesus' genealogy (Matthew 1:5) — worth mentioning in What Happened Next

### Chapter 6 — Deborah
**Hero Trait:** Fearless Leadership

**Read:** Judges 4–5
*(Chapter 4: the narrative. Chapter 5: the Song of Deborah — one of the oldest texts in the Bible. At minimum read chapter 4.)*

**Story in brief:** Israel is being oppressed by a Canaanite king named Jabin and his military commander Sisera. Deborah is the judge of Israel — the only woman to hold this role — and a prophetess. She summons a military commander named Barak and gives him God's orders to go to battle. Barak refuses to go without her. She agrees to go — but tells him the honor of the victory will go to a woman. The battle is won. Sisera flees and is killed by a woman named Jael (tent peg through the head — do not sanitize this for kids, they will love it).

**The Turning Point:** Judges 4:9 — Barak says I won't go without you. Deborah doesn't argue or wait for permission. She says fine, let's go — and goes. Leadership that doesn't wait for someone else to act first.

**Here I Am angle:** Deborah was already doing what needed to be done before anyone asked her to. She didn't wait to be given a title or told she was allowed. She saw what was needed and led.

**Memory verse:** Judges 4:14 — *"Go! This is the day the LORD has given Sisera into your hands. Has not the LORD gone ahead of you?"*

**Notes for writing:**
- Deborah holds court under a palm tree (Judges 4:5) — vivid detail
- The tent peg scene (Judges 4:17–21) is graphic but absolutely true to the text. Kids will remember it. The book shouldn't pretend it didn't happen.
- The Song of Deborah (Judges 5) is one of the oldest pieces of Hebrew poetry in existence — worth a line about that in What Happened Next
- Deborah is introduced as "wife of Lappidoth" (4:4) — we know almost nothing about her husband, only her

## Part Three: Unlikely Heroes

### Chapter 7 — Esther
**Hero Trait:** Hidden Courage

**Read:** Esther 1–9
*(The whole book. Focus on chapters 2–7 for the narrative core.)*

**Story in brief:** A Jewish girl named Hadassah (her Hebrew name) is orphaned and raised by her older cousin Mordecai in Persia. She is chosen for the king's harem and eventually becomes Queen Esther — without revealing she is Jewish. When the king's official Haman convinces the king to sign a decree authorizing the destruction of all Jews in the empire, Mordecai tells Esther she must act. She has been hiding her identity. She approaches the king uninvited — a capital offense — reveals who she is, and appeals for her people. The king reverses the decree. Haman is executed on the gallows he built for Mordecai.

**The Turning Point:** Esther 4:16 — *"If I perish, I perish."* She stops hiding. She stops calculating the risk. She decides that who she is matters more than her safety.

**Here I Am angle:** There are moments when staying quiet about who you are is no longer an option. When hiding your faith, your values, or who you love costs someone else. Esther had everything to lose and acted anyway.

**Memory verse:** Esther 4:14 — *"And who knows but that you have come to your royal position for such a time as this?"*

**Notes for writing:**
- God is never mentioned by name anywhere in the Book of Esther — the only book of the Bible where this is true. His fingerprints are all over it.
- "Such a time as this" (4:14) is the most quoted line in the book — Mordecai's challenge to Esther
- Esther fasts for three days before approaching the king (4:16) — she prays even though the text never uses that word

### Chapter 8 — Caleb
**Hero Trait:** Patient Boldness

**Read:** Numbers 13–14, Joshua 14–15
*(Numbers 13–14: the twelve spies. Joshua 14: Caleb's request at 85. Short readings, high impact.)*

**Story in brief:** Moses sends twelve spies into Canaan to scout the land God has promised. They come back with a divided report: ten spies say the people there are giants and Israel can't win. Two spies — Caleb and Joshua — say God has given it to them and they can take it. The Israelites believe the ten. As punishment, that entire generation (except Caleb and Joshua) will wander the wilderness until they die — never entering the Promised Land. Forty-five years later, Caleb walks up to Joshua and asks for the hardest territory — the hill country the fearful spies had been most afraid of. He is eighty-five years old.

**The Turning Point:** Joshua 14:12 — *"Now give me this hill country that the LORD promised me that day."* Forty-five years of waiting. Not bitter. Not forgotten. Still ready.

**Here I Am angle:** It is never too late to claim what God promised you. And it is never too early to be the one person in the room who says we can do this when everyone else says we can't.

**Memory verse:** Joshua 14:12 — *"Now give me this hill country that the LORD promised me that day. You yourself heard then that the Anakites were there and their cities were large and fortified, but the LORD helping me, I will drive them out just as he said."*

**Notes for writing:**
- Numbers 13:30 — *"We should go up and take possession of the land, for we can certainly do it."* Caleb says this while everyone else is terrified. One voice.
- Caleb is described as one who "followed the LORD wholeheartedly" — this phrase appears four times in connection with him
- He's 85 and asking for the hardest fight. That's the whole chapter.
- His daughter Achsah also shows boldness (Joshua 15:18–19) — she asks her father for more land and springs of water. Worth a line.

### Chapter 9 — Rahab
**Hero Trait:** Unexpected Faith

**Read:** Joshua 2, Joshua 6:17–25, Matthew 1:1–6 (genealogy of Jesus)
*(Joshua 2: Rahab hides the spies. Joshua 6: the fall of Jericho and Rahab's rescue. Matthew 1 is brief — just shows where she ends up.)*

**Story in brief:** Joshua sends two spies into Jericho before the Israelite army arrives. They end up at the house of Rahab, a prostitute, which is built into the city wall. The king finds out and sends men to look for them. Rahab hides the spies under stalks of flax on her roof and sends the king's men on a false trail. Why? Because she has heard what God did for Israel in Egypt and at the Red Sea — and she believes it. She asks the spies to spare her family when Israel takes the city. They agree. She ties a red cord in her window. When Jericho falls, Rahab and her family are the only ones saved. She goes on to marry into the Israelite community and appears in the lineage of Jesus.

**The Turning Point:** Joshua 2:11 — *"The LORD your God is God in heaven above and on the earth below."* A Canaanite woman — an outsider, a person with a complicated past — makes this declaration before most of Israel does.

**Here I Am angle:** You don't need a perfect past, a religious family, or a clean record for God to use you. Rahab had none of those things. She had one moment of faith and she acted on it.

**Memory verse:** Joshua 2:11 — *"For the LORD your God is God in heaven above and on the earth below."*

**Notes for writing:**
- Rahab appears in Hebrews 11:31 — the Hall of Faith — listed alongside Abraham, Moses, and David
- She appears in Matthew 1:5 as the mother of Boaz — which makes her Ruth's mother-in-law and King David's great-great-grandmother
- The red cord in the window is a detail kids will latch onto — some scholars connect it to the Passover blood on the doorposts
- She is called a prostitute in the text (Joshua 2:1) — don't need to dwell on it but don't pretend it isn't there either; it's part of why her faith is so striking

## Part Four: Heroes of the Everyday

### Chapter 10 — Gideon
**Hero Trait:** Reluctant Warrior

**Read:** Judges 6–8
*(Focus on chapters 6–7 for the core story. Chapter 8 covers the aftermath.)*

**Story in brief:** Israel is being terrorized by the Midianites — crops destroyed, livestock stolen, people hiding in caves. Gideon is threshing wheat in a winepress (hiding it from the Midianites) when God's angel appears and calls him a "mighty warrior." Gideon's response: I am the least member of the weakest clan in the smallest tribe. He tests God twice with a fleece of wool (asking for dew on the fleece but not the ground, then the reverse). God is patient with both tests. Then God trims Gideon's army from 32,000 down to 300 — deliberately, so Israel cannot claim credit for the victory. Gideon's 300 men surround the Midianite camp at night, break clay jars revealing torches, blow trumpets, and the Midianite army destroys itself in the chaos.

**The Turning Point:** Judges 7:7 — God tells Gideon: 300 men. That's it. Not 32,000, not 10,000. The army is too big — if they win, they'll think they did it themselves. Gideon has to trust that God plus 300 is enough.

**Here I Am angle:** When you feel completely unqualified for what you've been asked to do — when you're the least, the smallest, the one hiding in the winepress — God often calls you first. Not because you're ready. Because He is.

**Memory verse:** Judges 6:14 — *"Go in the strength you have and save Israel out of Midian's hand. Am I not sending you?"*

**Notes for writing:**
- The fleece tests (Judges 6:36–40) are great for kids — Gideon basically asks God to prove it twice and God does both times without complaint
- Gideon's army is trimmed by how they drink water (Judges 7:4–8) — the ones who kneel vs. the ones who lap from their hands. 300 lap from their hands. Strange detail, kids love it.
- The torches in clay jars (Judges 7:16–20) — each man breaks his jar at the signal. The crash, the light, the trumpet blasts. Cinematic.
- Gideon later makes a golden ephod that leads Israel into idol worship (Judges 8:27) — important for the What Happened Next section, shows he wasn't perfect either

### Chapter 11 — Hannah
**Hero Trait:** Faithful Grief

**Read:** 1 Samuel 1–2
*(Two chapters. The whole story is there.)*

**Story in brief:** Hannah is one of two wives of a man named Elkanah. She is childless. The other wife, Peninnah, has children and uses this to provoke Hannah year after year. Hannah is in constant grief. At the annual trip to the temple at Shiloh, Hannah goes to the tabernacle and prays so intensely — silently, lips moving, no sound — that the priest Eli thinks she's drunk. She tells him she is not drunk; she is pouring out her soul to God. She has made a vow: if God gives her a son, she will dedicate him to God's service. God gives her Samuel. She follows through. She brings the child to Eli at the temple and leaves him there to grow up serving God. Her prayer in chapter 2 — the Magnificat of the Old Testament — is one of the great prayers in scripture.

**The Turning Point:** 1 Samuel 1:15 — *"I am a woman who is deeply troubled. I have not been drinking wine or beer; I was pouring out my soul to the LORD."* She stops suffering silently. She takes the pain — all of it — directly to God instead of carrying it alone.

**Here I Am angle:** When something feels deeply unfair and the hurt won't go away — Hannah shows us what to do with that. Not perform happiness. Not pretend it's fine. Take it to God exactly as it is.

**Memory verse:** 1 Samuel 1:27 — *"I prayed for this child, and the LORD has granted me what I asked of him."*

**Notes for writing:**
- Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2:1–10 is almost word-for-word mirrored by Mary's Magnificat in Luke 1:46–55 — worth mentioning in What Happened Next
- The child she gives up is Samuel, who goes on to anoint Saul and David as kings of Israel
- Eli initially misreads her (thinks she's drunk) — the priest of God misses what God is actually doing right in front of him. Hannah has to correct him.
- She goes on to have five more children (1 Samuel 2:21) after giving Samuel up

### Chapter 12 — Nehemiah
**Hero Trait:** Practical Faith

**Read:** Nehemiah 1–6
*(Chapters 1–2: the prayer and the request. Chapters 3–4: the build under opposition. Chapter 6: the wall is finished. Focus especially on 1–2 and 4.)*

**Story in brief:** Nehemiah is the cupbearer to the Persian king Artaxerxes — a position of trust and proximity to power. He receives word that Jerusalem's walls are still in ruins decades after the Babylonian destruction. He weeps. He fasts. He prays for months. Then the king notices his sadness and asks what's wrong. Nehemiah prays a quick silent prayer — right there in the throne room — and then makes his request: let me go rebuild Jerusalem. The king says yes and gives him everything he needs. Nehemiah organizes the people of Jerusalem to rebuild the wall in sections. Enemies ridicule them, threaten them, plot against them. Nehemiah arms the workers — one hand holding a tool, one hand holding a weapon. The wall is completed in 52 days.

**The Turning Point:** Nehemiah 2:4–5 — The king asks what he wants. The text says: *"Then I prayed to the God of heaven, and I answered the king."* A one-breath prayer in the middle of a royal audience. Then he asks. He was afraid but he asked anyway.

**Here I Am angle:** Big things get done one section of wall at a time. Nehemiah didn't rebuild Jerusalem in a day — he organized people, kept going under opposition, worked and prayed at the same time, and finished what he started. That is what faithful work looks like.

**Memory verse:** Nehemiah 4:14 — *"Don't be afraid of them. Remember the Lord, who is great and awesome, and fight for your families, your sons and your daughters, your wives and your homes."*

**Notes for writing:**
- Nehemiah's opening prayer (1:4–11) is one of the most honest prayers in scripture — he confesses Israel's sin, reminds God of his own promises, and asks for help. Good model for kids.
- The wall-building in chapter 3 lists every family and every section they built — the whole city participated
- Sanballat and Tobiah (the main opponents) try mockery first, then threats, then a fake meeting designed to lure Nehemiah away. He doesn't go. *"I am doing a great work and I cannot come down."* (6:3)
- Wall completed in 52 days (6:15). Enemies lose their confidence because they recognize the work was done with God's help (6:16).
