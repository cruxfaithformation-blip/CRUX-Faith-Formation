1
The Sequel

Acts of the Apostles is Luke's second book. His Gospel told you what Jesus did. Acts tells you what happened next — what happened when a group of frightened, scattered disciples decided that the resurrection was real and started acting like it.

The result was one of the most improbable stories in human history. Within three centuries, a movement that began with twelve fishermen, tax collectors, and outcasts in a small Roman province had spread across the entire known world and transformed the Roman Empire itself. Acts is the story of how that started.

But before we get to the triumph, notice where Acts begins: in an upper room in Jerusalem. The disciples are hiding. Their leader was executed three weeks ago. They have no money, no power, no political connections, and no plan. They are waiting — because Jesus told them to wait.

Luke's Two-Volume Work

The Gospel of Luke and Acts of the Apostles were written by the same author as a single, connected work. Luke addresses both books to someone named Theophilus — probably a wealthy Roman patron who funded their writing. Together they form the longest contribution to the New Testament by any single author.

The question Acts is answering is simple: if Jesus rose from the dead, so what? What does that actually change? The answer turns out to be: everything — starting with the people who least expected it.

2
Pentecost

Fifty days after Easter — on the Jewish feast of Pentecost — something happened to the disciples in that upper room that they couldn't have planned or manufactured. Luke describes it as a sound like a violent wind filling the house, and what looked like tongues of fire resting on each of them. Then they started speaking in languages they didn't know.

The city of Jerusalem was packed with Jewish pilgrims from across the Roman world — people from Egypt, Rome, Persia, Asia Minor — each speaking their own language. When they heard the commotion and came to investigate, every single one of them heard the disciples speaking in their own native language.

Acts 2:7–8

"Utterly amazed, they asked: 'Aren't all these who are speaking Galileans? Then how is it that each of us hears them in our native language?'"

This is the moment the Church begins. Not with a strategy document or a building program or a committee vote — with a wind and fire from heaven that nobody planned and everybody witnessed.

Peter — the same Peter who denied knowing Jesus three times a few weeks earlier — stands up in front of this crowd and delivers a sermon. He explains everything: Jesus, the crucifixion, the resurrection, what it all means. Three thousand people are baptized that day. The Church goes from 120 people hiding in a room to 3,120 people before sunset.

What Is the Holy Spirit?

Christians believe the Holy Spirit is the third person of the Trinity — not a force or an energy, but a person: God dwelling within and among his people. At Pentecost, the disciples receive what Jesus had promised: that after his departure, the Father would send the Spirit to be their guide, advocate, and power. The Church doesn't run on human ability. It runs on this.

Reflection

Peter goes from denying Jesus by a fire in a courtyard to preaching about him in front of thousands — in weeks. What do you think changed in him? What would it take for something like that to change in you?

3
The First Community

What does a community built on the resurrection actually look like? Luke gives us a snapshot in Acts chapter 2 — a description of the early Jerusalem church that has fascinated and challenged Christians ever since.

Acts 2:44–47

"All the believers were together and had everything in common. They sold property and possessions to give to anyone who had need. Every day they continued to meet together in the temple courts. They broke bread in their homes and ate together with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all the people."

This community is doing four things: learning (the apostles' teaching), fellowship (being together), breaking bread (the Eucharist), and prayer. These four things become the core pattern of Christian life — and they're still the four pillars of every healthy parish today.

The "breaking of bread" is worth slowing down on. This isn't just sharing meals together, though it is that too. From the very beginning, Christians understood that when Jesus said "do this in memory of me," he meant something that had to be done — regularly, together, in a specific way. The Eucharist is not an addition to Christian life. It's the center of it.

"They had everything in common" — not because they were forced to, but because when you actually believe in the resurrection, your grip on your possessions loosens.

Discussion

  • The early Christians sold their possessions to help anyone in need. Does that sound radical to you? Impossible? Inspiring? All three?
  • The four pillars — teaching, fellowship, breaking bread, prayer — which of these does your parish or faith community do well? Which feels weakest?

4
Persecution

It doesn't take long for the authorities to notice what's happening. The same religious leaders who had Jesus executed are watching the apostles heal people, preach in the Temple, and draw enormous crowds. They arrest Peter and John. They demand they stop speaking in Jesus's name.

Peter's response is one of the most remarkable moments in the whole book:

Acts 4:19–20

"Which is right in God's eyes: to listen to you, or to him? You be the judges! As for us, we cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard."

This is Peter — the man who was afraid of a servant girl recognizing him by a fire in a courtyard just weeks ago. Now he's in front of the Sanhedrin, the highest religious court in Israel, and he's telling them calmly: we're not going to stop. The authorities are stunned. They let them go because they can't figure out how to punish them without the crowd turning on them.

The persecution escalates. Stephen — one of the first deacons — is arrested and gives a blistering speech before the council. He's dragged out of the city and stoned to death, becoming the first Christian martyr. Luke notes that a young man named Saul was standing there, watching, approving of Stephen's death. That name will matter soon.

Martyr

The word "martyr" comes from the Greek word for witness. Stephen doesn't just die for his beliefs — he dies bearing witness to what he has seen and heard. As he's being stoned, he prays the same prayer Jesus prayed from the cross: "Lord, do not hold this sin against them." His death is not a defeat. In Acts, every persecution ends up spreading the faith further.

5
The Road to Damascus

After Stephen's death, Saul becomes the Church's most dangerous enemy. He goes house to house, dragging Christians out and throwing them in prison. He gets authorization from the high priest to travel to Damascus and arrest Christians there too. He is, by his own later admission, trying to destroy the Church.

Then something happens on the road to Damascus that Saul will spend the rest of his life trying to explain:

Acts 9:3–5

"As he neared Damascus on his journey, suddenly a light from heaven flashed around him. He fell to the ground and heard a voice say to him, 'Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?' 'Who are you, Lord?' Saul asked. 'I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting,' he replied."

Saul goes blind for three days. He doesn't eat or drink. A disciple in Damascus named Ananias — understandably terrified of Saul's reputation — is told by God to go heal him. Ananias goes. Saul receives his sight back, is baptized, and immediately begins preaching in the synagogues that Jesus is the Son of God. Everyone who hears him is astonished.

This conversion is one of the most theologically significant events in the New Testament. Saul — who becomes Paul — is not a sympathizer who slowly came around. He was an enemy. His conversion is a demonstration that the resurrection changes everything, even for people who hate it. It also gives the Church its greatest theologian and missionary.

Reflection

Jesus asks Saul: "Why do you persecute me?" — not "why do you persecute my followers?" What does that tell you about how Jesus sees his relationship to the Church?

6
The Gentiles

The early Church begins as a Jewish movement — all of the first Christians are Jewish, and most assume the faith is for Jews only. Then God does something that surprises everyone, including Peter.

Peter receives a strange vision: a sheet lowered from heaven containing all kinds of animals that Jewish law declares unclean. A voice tells him to eat. Peter refuses — he's never eaten anything impure in his life. The voice responds: "Do not call anything impure that God has made clean." This happens three times.

Almost immediately, messengers arrive asking Peter to come to the house of a Roman centurion named Cornelius — a Gentile, a non-Jew. Peter goes. He preaches. The Holy Spirit falls on everyone in the room — including the Gentiles — before anyone has even been baptized. Peter's conclusion is unavoidable:

Acts 10:34–35

"I now realize how true it is that God does not show favoritism but accepts from every nation the one who fears him and does what is right."

This moment cracks the Church wide open. The Gospel is not for one ethnic group or one culture. It is, as Jesus said, for "the ends of the earth." The decision to welcome Gentiles — confirmed at the Council of Jerusalem in Acts 15 — is the moment Christianity becomes a world religion rather than a Jewish sect.

"The Holy Spirit fell on them just as he had fallen on us at the beginning." Peter can't argue with what God has already done.

— Acts 11:15 (paraphrased)

7
Paul's Mission

The second half of Acts follows Paul across the Roman world — three enormous missionary journeys covering thousands of miles, mostly on foot or by ship. He plants churches in major Roman cities: Corinth, Ephesus, Philippi, Thessalonica, Athens. He preaches in synagogues, marketplaces, courtrooms, and prisons. He is beaten with rods, shipwrecked, stoned and left for dead, imprisoned multiple times.

In Athens — the intellectual capital of the ancient world — he stands before the Areopagus, the city's council of philosophers, and delivers one of the most remarkable sermons in Acts:

Acts 17:22–23

"People of Athens! I see that in every way you are very religious. For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: TO AN UNKNOWN GOD. So you are ignorant of the very thing you worship — and this is what I am going to proclaim to you."

Paul doesn't condemn the Athenians for their religious searching. He starts where they are and moves toward Christ. He quotes their own poets back to them. Some mock him when he mentions the resurrection; some want to hear more; a few believe. It's not a mass conversion — but it's an honest one. Paul adapts his approach without changing his message.

Why Does This Matter Now?

Paul's Athens sermon is a model for how Christians engage a culture that doesn't share their assumptions. He doesn't start by condemning what they believe. He starts by finding what's true in it and building from there. Every generation of Christians has to figure out how to do what Paul did — speak to the culture they're actually in, not the one they wish they were in.

Reflection

Paul finds an "unknown God" altar and uses it as an opening. What equivalent "altars" do you see in today's culture — things people are searching for that point, without knowing it, toward Christ?

8
Rome — and Beyond

Acts ends with Paul in Rome — under house arrest, chained to a Roman soldier, awaiting trial before Caesar. And yet Luke's final image of him is this:

Acts 28:30–31

"For two whole years Paul stayed there in his own rented house and welcomed all who came to see him. He proclaimed the kingdom of God and taught about the Lord Jesus Christ — with all boldness and without hindrance!"

The last word of Acts in Greek is akolutos — unhindered. A man in chains, in the capital of the empire that persecuted his movement, is preaching the kingdom of God without hindrance. Luke ends his book right there — not with Paul's trial, not with his execution, not with a tidy conclusion. Just: still preaching, still unhindered.

This ending, like Mark's, is deliberate. The story isn't over. Acts doesn't end because the mission is complete — it ends because the mission continues, in the reader's own time and place. The Church that began with wind and fire in a Jerusalem upper room is the same Church that exists today. You are the next chapter of Acts.

The book is called Acts of the Apostles. The unwritten chapters are acts of everyone who came after them — including you.

Discussion Questions

  • Acts begins with disciples hiding in a room and ends with Paul preaching boldly in Rome under arrest. What drove that transformation? What's the through-line?
  • Every time the Church is persecuted in Acts, the faith spreads further. Why do you think opposition seems to strengthen rather than weaken the early Church?
  • Paul says to the Athenians: "You worship what you don't know." Where do you see that in the people around you today — people searching for something they can't name?
  • Luke ends Acts mid-story, still in motion. What do you think your chapter of Acts looks like?

Final Reflection

The Holy Spirit is described in Acts as wind, fire, and a presence that shows up in prisons, shipwrecks, and ordinary conversations. Where have you seen or felt something like that in your own life — moments where something bigger than circumstance seemed to be at work?
