Why Is There Suffering?

The oldest question. The hardest question. Before we can offer any answer, we have to take the question seriously.

A The Question B The Cross as Answer

If there is a God, and he is good, and he is powerful — why does a child get cancer? Why do tsunamis kill thousands of innocents? Why do good people suffer and cruel people flourish?

This is not a question invented by atheists to make Christians uncomfortable. It is a question that sits at the center of the Bible itself. Job asks it. The Psalms ask it. Jesus asks it — from the Cross.

If you're sitting with this question, you're in good company. This module won't pretend the answer is easy. What it will do is take the question seriously — and then offer what the Catholic tradition has to say, which is stranger and more honest than most people expect.

Why the Question Has Weight

The philosopher's version of the problem goes like this: If God is all-powerful, he could prevent suffering. If God is all-good, he would want to prevent suffering. Suffering exists. Therefore, an all-powerful, all-good God does not exist.

This argument has serious force. It is not enough to respond with platitudes. "God has a plan" or "everything happens for a reason" — said too quickly, to someone in real pain — is not theology. It is dismissal dressed up as comfort.

The first thing Catholic tradition asks us to do is sit with the weight of the question. Not to explain it away. Not to solve it with a formula. To feel what is actually being asked.

The question of suffering is, at its root, a question about whether love is real. If a God of love created this world, what does this world say about that love?

— A central tension of the Book of Job
Three Kinds of Suffering

Not all suffering is the same, and collapsing them together makes the problem harder to think about clearly. Catholic theology distinguishes between three broad categories:

⚡

Moral Evil

Suffering caused by human choices — violence, betrayal, injustice, war, cruelty. A person hurts another person. This suffering has a clear cause: human freedom, used badly. God did not will the Holocaust. Human beings did.

🌊

Natural Evil

Suffering that comes from the physical world — earthquakes, disease, floods, genetic illness. No human chose this. It just happens. This is the harder kind to account for, and the one that tests faith most sharply.

🕯

Redemptive Suffering

A category Catholic theology takes seriously that secular frameworks often don't: suffering that is somehow, mysteriously, transformed. Not explained away — transformed. This is the direction Part B will take.

What Philosophy Can and Can't Do

Theologians and philosophers have offered responses to the problem of suffering for centuries. Some of them are genuinely useful. But it's important to be honest about what they accomplish — and what they don't.

What arguments can do
  • Show that suffering is logically compatible with a good God
  • Explain why free will requires the possibility of evil
  • Challenge the assumption that a good God would eliminate all pain
  • Demonstrate that the problem isn't as simple as the objection sounds
What arguments can't do
  • Make suffering hurt less
  • Justify a specific child's death in a specific accident
  • Produce the feeling of being held by God in the dark
  • Replace what is actually needed: presence, not explanation

The most important theodicy — the most important "defense of God" in the face of suffering — is not a philosophical argument. It is the Cross. That is where Catholic theology goes, and we'll get there in Part B.

But first: the free will argument, because it's the most common response, and it's worth understanding what it actually says.

The Free Will Argument

The most common philosophical response to the problem of moral evil goes like this: God gave human beings genuine freedom. Real freedom — not the kind that only permits right choices, but the kind that can genuinely choose wrong. Without that, love would be impossible. A programmed response to God is not love. It is a machine running its code.

The problem with creating a world with real freedom is that real freedom can be used to do real harm. God, on this account, is not the author of evil — he is the author of freedom, and freedom has been used against itself and against others throughout human history.

This accounts well for moral evil. It is less satisfying for natural evil — the child with leukemia didn't suffer because someone made a bad choice. The tradition has responses here too, but they are more speculative: that a physical world capable of sustaining life requires the same natural laws that can also produce earthquakes; that this world is not the final word; that eternity changes the calculus in ways we cannot yet see.

These are honest responses. They are not complete ones. And Catholic intellectual honesty acknowledges that.

An honest admission

No philosophical argument fully explains why this suffering happened to this person. Theodicy can show that God's existence is not logically disproven by the existence of suffering. It cannot provide a specific justification for every specific grief.

The Catholic tradition does not pretend otherwise. The Book of Job ends not with an explanation, but with an encounter. God does not explain Job's suffering to him. God shows up.

That distinction — between explanation and presence — is the heart of where Part B is going.

What Job Actually Teaches

The Book of Job is one of the most extraordinary texts in the Bible — and one of the most misunderstood. Popular religion often turns Job into a story about patience rewarded: Job suffered, Job was patient, Job got everything back. That reading flattens something much stranger and more honest.

Job argues with God. He demands an answer. He rejects the explanations his friends offer — that he must have sinned, that there must be a reason, that he should just accept it. God, at the end of the book, tells Job's friends that they were wrong and Job was right. The man who wrestled and demanded and refused easy comfort was closer to the truth than the ones offering tidy explanations.

God's response to Job is not an answer. It is a presence. An overwhelming, humbling, awe-inducing presence that doesn't explain the suffering — but somehow, mysteriously, is enough.

That pattern — presence rather than explanation — runs through the whole of Catholic theology on suffering. It reaches its fullest expression in one place.

"Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?"

— Job 38:4 · God's answer to Job

This is not God dismissing Job. It is God inviting Job into a reality larger than the frame Job has been using. The suffering hasn't been explained. But something has shifted. Job has encountered the living God — not a theory about God, not a defense of God, but God himself.

That is the move Part B makes. Not a philosophical argument. A crucified God.
