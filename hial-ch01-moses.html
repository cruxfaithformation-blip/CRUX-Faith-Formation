Moses

The Reluctant Hero Who Ran Toward the Fire

Reluctant Courage
△ Illustration · Moses at the Burning Bush
The Hook

The bush was on fire, but it wasn't burning up.

Moses had been a shepherd for forty years. He knew what fire looked like, what it did to dry brush in the middle of a desert summer. But this fire was different. The branches still had their leaves. The flames were alive and they weren't eating anything.

He walked closer. That was probably his first mistake. Or maybe his best one.

The Story

Moses' story starts before he was born. The king of Egypt had issued an order: kill every Hebrew baby boy. His mother didn't obey. She built a small basket, waterproofed it with tar and pitch, and placed her son in the Nile River, trusting God to take it from there. Pharaoh's own daughter found the baby floating in the reeds. She named him Moses and raised him as a prince of Egypt.

Decades later, Moses was hiding from his past in the wilderness when God showed up in that burning bush. God gave Moses the most important mission in Israelite history: go back to Egypt, face the most powerful ruler in the world, and set the Hebrew people free. Moses had five answers ready. None of them were yes.

"I'm nobody." God answered: I will be with you.
"They won't believe me." God answered: I'll give you signs.
"I can't speak well." God answered: I'll give you words.
"Please, just send someone else." God answered: I'm sending you.

Then Moses ran out of excuses. He went.

What followed was one of the most dramatic showdowns in history. Moses stood before Pharaoh again and again and said, "Let my people go." Pharaoh said no. Then God sent ten plagues on Egypt, one after another, each one more severe than the last, until Pharaoh finally agreed. When he changed his mind and sent his army after the Israelites, Moses stretched his staff over the Red Sea. The water split. Two million people walked through on dry ground. And when the Egyptian army followed, the water came back.

Moses led his people through the desert for forty years. It wasn't easy. At one point, Moses got so frustrated that he disobeyed a direct instruction from God, and because of that, God told him he would never enter the Promised Land himself. Moses led a nation to the edge of everything they had hoped for, then handed the work to someone else. He never walked through the door he helped build.

The Turning Point

Moses reached the end of his excuses at the burning bush. He had made five of them, one right after another, and God had answered every single one with patience. Then Moses said the most honest thing he could think of: "Please, Lord. Just send someone else."

God didn't argue. He didn't shame him. He just said: "I will be with you. That's the answer to every excuse you have."

Moses went.

What Happened Next

The ten plagues. The parting of the Red Sea. Millions of people walking out of slavery for the first time in generations. Forty years of leading a nation through the wilderness. The Ten Commandments, received on a mountain while thunder shook the ground. Moses became one of the most important figures in all of human history, and he began that story by telling God he wasn't the right person for the job.

Here I Am Moment

God doesn't call the equipped. He calls the willing, and then equips them along the way.

Moses' excuses were real. His fear was real. His feeling of not being enough was completely real. But God's answer to every single one of those feelings was the same thing: "I will be with you."

Whatever you feel like you can't do, that is not the last word. Moses felt exactly the same way, standing in front of a burning bush with no idea what came next. And then he went anyway. And the world was never the same.

Dig Deeper
1

Moses made five excuses before he finally said yes. What is an excuse you use when something feels too hard or too scary?

2

God was patient with every single one of Moses' excuses and answered each one. What does that tell you about how God responds to our doubts and fears?

3

Moses never got to enter the Promised Land himself, but he spent forty years leading others toward it. What do you think it means to do something important even if you don't get to see how it ends?
