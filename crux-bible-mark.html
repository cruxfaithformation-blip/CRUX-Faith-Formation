1
The Fastest Gospel

Mark's Gospel is the shortest of the four — and the fastest. The word immediately appears 41 times. Jesus doesn't sit down and give long speeches. He moves. He acts. He heals. He argues. He silences storms. Mark writes like someone who has urgent news and no time to waste.

This study is going to work through Mark's Gospel asking one question on every page: who is this man? That's the question his disciples ask in chapter 4, sitting in a boat after Jesus has just told a violent storm to be quiet — and it was. That's the question the whole Gospel is trying to answer.

Before we begin, a quick note: Mark was not an apostle. He wasn't one of the twelve. Most scholars believe he was a close companion of Peter, and that his Gospel is essentially Peter's eyewitness account — urgent, vivid, full of details only someone who was there would notice. When Mark says the disciples "looked at each other in terror," he's reporting what Peter actually remembered.

Why Mark First?

Mark was almost certainly written before the other Gospels — probably between AD 65–70, when Peter was still alive. Matthew and Luke both use Mark as a source. John writes last and goes deepest theologically. Reading Mark first is like hearing the news before anyone has had time to reflect on it.

2
Immediately

Open your Bible to Mark 1. Read the first twenty verses. Count how many times the word "immediately" appears. By verse 20, Jesus has been baptized, tempted in the desert, and called four fishermen away from their nets — and all of it happens in less than one chapter.

This pace is deliberate. Mark wants you to feel the urgency. Something has happened in history that cannot wait. The kingdom of God is breaking into the world right now, and everyone in Jesus's path has to decide what to do about him.

Mk 1:16–18

"As Jesus walked beside the Sea of Galilee, he saw Simon and his brother Andrew casting a net into the lake, for they were fishermen. 'Come, follow me,' Jesus said, 'and I will send you out to fish for people.' At once they left their nets and followed him."

At once. They left everything. No deliberation, no two weeks' notice. Mark doesn't explain why. He just reports what happened. And what happened was that two fishermen — not scholars, not religious leaders, not important people — dropped what they were doing and followed a stranger.

The key question isn't why they followed. It's: what did they see in him that made it an obvious decision?

Reflection

If someone came to you today and said "follow me," what would it take for you to actually go? What does that say about what Jesus must have been like in person?

3
With Authority

One of the first things people notice about Jesus in Mark's Gospel is that he teaches differently. The religious leaders of his day would say "Rabbi So-and-So said this, and Rabbi Such-and-Such disagreed." They argued about tradition. They cited sources.

Jesus just says what's true. "You have heard it said — but I say to you." No citations. No committee approval. Just: this is how things are. The crowds notice immediately.

Mk 1:27

"The people were all so amazed that they asked each other, 'What is this? A new teaching — and with authority! He even gives orders to impure spirits and they obey him.'"

The miracles and the teaching go together in Mark. He doesn't just tell people what's right — he has the power to actually change things. He doesn't just say "be healed." People are healed. He doesn't just say "be free." Demons leave.

Mark is making a point: Jesus has authority over the natural world, the spiritual world, and the moral world. That combination doesn't fit any normal category. The people asking "what is this?" are asking the right question.

"He was not like the scribes. He spoke as one who had the right."

— Mark 1:22 (paraphrased)

4
Who He Chose

Look at who Jesus spends time with in Mark's Gospel. A man with a demon. A leper. A paralyzed man whose friends broke through a roof to get him close. A woman who had been bleeding for twelve years and was considered unclean. A dead girl. Blind Bartimaeus screaming on the side of the road while the crowd told him to shut up.

Every single one of these people was, in some way, excluded. Untouchable. Not the kind of person a respectable rabbi would associate with in public. Jesus not only associates with them — he makes them the point. He crosses every social line to get to them.

Mk 2:16–17

"When the teachers of the law who were Pharisees saw him eating with the sinners and tax collectors, they asked his disciples: 'Why does he eat with tax collectors and sinners?' On hearing this, Jesus said to them, 'It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick. I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners.'"

This is a direct shot at the religious establishment. The Pharisees were the most respected religious leaders in Israel. They kept all the rules. Jesus is saying: you've entirely missed the point of the rules. The point was always the people the rules were supposed to serve.

This gets him in trouble almost immediately — and he knows it will. But he doesn't stop.

Reflection

Who in your school or community would be the modern equivalent of the people Jesus was spending time with? What would it cost to be seen with them?

5
The Question

In Mark chapter 8, Jesus asks his disciples the most important question in the Gospel — maybe the most important question in history:

Mk 8:27–29

"Jesus and his disciples went on to the villages around Caesarea Philippi. On the way he asked them, 'Who do people say I am?' They replied, 'Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, one of the prophets.' 'But what about you?' he asked. 'Who do you say I am?' Peter answered, 'You are the Messiah.'"

This question sits at the exact center of Mark's Gospel — both structurally and theologically. Everything before it has been building to this point. Everything after it is the consequence of Peter's answer.

Jesus doesn't let anyone stay neutral. The crowd has opinions — he's John the Baptist, or Elijah, or a prophet. All respectful answers. All wrong. But the disciples have been with him for months. They've seen things no one else has seen. So Jesus presses: but what about you?

Peter says: Messiah. The anointed one. The one who was supposed to come and set everything right. It's the right answer — but as the next few chapters show, Peter doesn't fully understand what it means. And neither would we.

What Does "Messiah" Mean?

The Hebrew word Messiah (Greek: Christ) means "anointed one." In Jewish tradition, this referred to a coming king who would deliver Israel. Most people expected a political or military leader. What they got was something far stranger and more radical: a king who rode into Jerusalem on a donkey and died on a cross.

6
The Road to Jerusalem

After Peter's confession, everything changes. Jesus begins telling his disciples what's going to happen. Three times — in chapters 8, 9, and 10 — he tells them plainly: the Son of Man is going to be handed over, killed, and will rise again on the third day.

Three times they don't understand. Or they don't want to. Peter, right after correctly identifying Jesus as the Messiah, pulls him aside and tells him this absolutely cannot happen. Jesus turns on him: "Get behind me, Satan."

The disciples are thinking about who gets to sit at Jesus's right hand in the kingdom. They're arguing about which of them is the greatest. Jesus keeps trying to tell them what kingdom he's actually bringing — one where the greatest is the servant of all.

Mk 10:45

"For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."

This is the operating logic of Jesus's entire ministry: power expressed through service and sacrifice. It was completely unintelligible to the culture of his day — and in many ways it still is. He's not overthinking it as a strategy. He means it literally. He's going to Jerusalem to die, and he knows it.

"They were on their way up to Jerusalem, with Jesus leading the way, and the disciples were astonished, while those who followed were afraid."

— Mark 10:32

7
The Cross

Mark's account of the crucifixion is brutal and spare. There are no long speeches from the cross — just one cry:

Mk 15:34

"At three in the afternoon Jesus cried out in a loud voice: 'Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?' (which means 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?')"

This is a quotation of Psalm 22 — a psalm that begins in desolation and ends in vindication. Jesus isn't just expressing despair; he's praying a psalm his listeners would have known. But the raw anguish of it is real. The Son of God, on a cross, feeling abandoned by his Father.

Immediately after Jesus dies, two things happen that Mark places side by side: the veil in the Temple — the curtain separating ordinary people from the presence of God — tears in two. And a Roman soldier standing at the cross says something no Jewish person in the crowd has said in the entire Gospel:

Mk 15:39

"And when the centurion, who stood there in front of Jesus, saw how he died, he said, 'Surely this man was the Son of God!'"

A pagan soldier. A representative of the empire that just executed Jesus. He's the first person in Mark's Gospel to say out loud what the whole Gospel has been trying to show. Not the religious leaders. Not the disciples. A Roman centurion who saw how he died.

Reflection

Why do you think Mark places these two events together — the Temple veil tearing and the centurion's confession? What is he trying to say about what the cross means?

8
The Empty Tomb

Mark's original ending — most scholars believe — stops at chapter 16, verse 8. Three women go to the tomb on Sunday morning to anoint Jesus's body. They find the stone rolled away. A young man in white tells them Jesus has risen and is going ahead of them to Galilee. And then:

Mk 16:8

"Trembling and bewildered, the women went out and fled from the tomb. They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid."

That's it. That's where Mark ends. No appearance of the risen Jesus. No triumphant ending. Just women running in fear from an empty tomb and a message they haven't yet delivered.

This ending is either a disaster or a masterstroke. Most scholars think it's deliberate. Mark ends his Gospel the way he began it — in the middle of things, urgent, unresolved. The story isn't over. The message still needs to be delivered. Someone still has to go to Galilee.

The question Mark leaves hanging is the same one he started with: who is this man? He has given you everything he has. The answer is waiting for you in Galilee.

Discussion Questions

  • Why do you think Mark ends his Gospel the way he does — with women fleeing in fear rather than a triumphant appearance?
  • Of all the people Jesus encounters in Mark — the leper, the paralyzed man, blind Bartimaeus, the centurion — who stands out most to you, and why?
  • The disciples keep failing to understand Jesus throughout Mark's Gospel. Does that make them more relatable or less? What does it say about who Jesus chooses?
  • If you had to answer Jesus's question — "But who do you say I am?" — what would you say right now, honestly?

Final Reflection

After working through Mark's Gospel, what do you understand about Jesus now that you didn't before? What question is still unanswered for you?
