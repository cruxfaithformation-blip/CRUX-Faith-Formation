Picking up where Part A left off

Part A covered the man. His life, his teaching, the people he chose, what his enemies and friends thought of him. If you've made it this far, something in that held your attention.

Part B asks the harder question. Not whether Jesus was remarkable; most people across history have conceded that much. The real question is whether the claim he made about himself holds up. Because if it does, it changes everything. And if it doesn't, the most influential person in human history was either deeply deluded or deliberately dishonest. There is no comfortable middle position. That is not a Catholic assertion. It follows directly from what Jesus actually said.

The claim itself

Jesus made statements no Jewish teacher of his era would have made without knowing exactly how they would land.

Jn 10:30 I and the Father are one.
Jn 8:58 Before Abraham was, I am.
Jn 14:9 Whoever has seen me has seen the Father.

He accepted worship on multiple occasions. He claimed authority to forgive sins, which his audience immediately understood as a claim belonging to God alone. That is why they accused him of blasphemy rather than mere arrogance.

The people who heard him understood exactly what he was saying. That is why they picked up stones (John 10:31). That is why the charge at his trial was blasphemy, not sedition. Pilate found no political crime. The religious authorities were not troubled by his politics. They were troubled by who he said he was.

The resurrection: the historical case

The resurrection of Jesus is not a claim invented by the medieval Church or embellished over centuries. It is the earliest claim in the entire Christian tradition. Paul's letter to the Corinthians, written roughly twenty years after the crucifixion, records a list of resurrection appearances: to Peter, to the twelve, to more than five hundred people at once, most of whom Paul notes were still alive when he was writing and available to be questioned (1 Corinthians 15:3-8). This is not the language of legend. This is a man pointing at living witnesses.

Historians who study the resurrection, including skeptical ones, generally agree on four facts that any credible explanation has to account for.

Fact 1
The tomb was empty. No one in Jerusalem disputed this, including the authorities who had every reason to produce the body.
Fact 2
The disciples claimed to have seen Jesus alive after his death, in multiple appearances, across multiple locations.
Fact 3
Those claims transformed a group of terrified fugitives into people willing to die for what they had witnessed.
Fact 4
The movement spread with extraordinary speed through the very city where the crucifixion had just happened, within weeks of the event.

The question is not whether these things happened. They are as well attested as almost anything from that era. The question is what explains them. The two most common alternatives, that the disciples stole the body or that they were hallucinating, both fall apart under examination. Mass hallucinations do not produce consistent detailed accounts across multiple independent witnesses. And people who steal a body do not then die rather than admit they stole it.

4
Liar, lunatic, Lord, or legend

C.S. Lewis framed the problem plainly in Mere Christianity. A man who says the things Jesus said leaves you very few options. He is either a liar who knew he was not God and claimed otherwise, a lunatic who genuinely believed something that was not true, or exactly who he said he was.

Lewis was careful about what he was ruling out. He was not ruling out the possibility that Jesus was wrong. He was ruling out the possibility that Jesus was simply a great moral teacher who meant well. A man who claims to be God cannot also be merely a great moral teacher. The claim itself is either true or it destroys the credibility of everything else he said. You cannot keep the ethics and discard the identity. They come as one package.

Liar
He knew he was not God and deliberately deceived people. But a liar does not go to the Cross for a claim he knows is false, and his closest followers did not die protecting a story they knew was invented.
Lunatic
He genuinely believed something that was not true. But a lunatic does not produce the most searching moral teaching in human history, maintain the psychological consistency the Gospels portray, or construct arguments two thousand years of scholarship has not managed to unseat.
Legend
The claims were invented by later followers. But the claims appear in the earliest sources, written while eyewitnesses were still alive. If they were invented, they were invented immediately, in the same city where the events happened, among people who could have disputed them.
Lord
He is exactly who he said he was. Not as a certainty anyone is required to accept on the spot, but as the conclusion that accounts for the most evidence with the fewest problems.

The myth theory requires a conspiracy of invention so fast and so total that it creates more problems than it solves. Which brings you back to three serious options. And the evidence, taken honestly, keeps pointing in the same direction.

The leap

No matter what anyone tells you, every Catholic has at one point questioned their faith. Maybe not intentionally. Maybe in a moment of frustration or despair. But we have all felt it. We have all asked some version of the same question: if so many people across history have not believed, who is to say that those who do are right?

The answer is that faith was never about certainty in numbers. Jesus addressed this directly:

Jn 20:29 Have you believed because you have seen me? Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed.

We may never know the whole truth until the day we meet our maker and get to ask Jesus those questions ourselves. But not being able to understand how God does what he does is not an excuse to abandon faith that he has done it. We have historical evidence pointing to Jesus as the Messiah the Old Testament promised. We have testimony of miracles he performed, including from non-biblical writers who acknowledged them while attributing them to sorcery rather than divinity, because they could not explain them any other way. We have miracles that still happen today in his name, documented and witnessed across the world.

The proof is all around us. The leap comes when you have not yet witnessed one yourself.

But consider this. As humans, almost everything we believe requires some leap of faith. The Big Bang. The age of the universe. The shape of the earth beneath your feet. We take those leaps every day, trusting what others have witnessed, studied, and verified. We believe things we cannot personally see because the evidence from those who could see is strong enough to earn that trust.

The only question that remains is whether you can make that same leap for a God who gave his one and only son as a sacrifice for you.

What the Church teaches

The Catholic Church teaches that Jesus Christ is the Second Person of the Trinity, fully God and fully human, not half of each but both completely and simultaneously. This is the doctrine of the Incarnation, defined at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD and restated at every Mass: "God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten not made, consubstantial with the Father."

The Church further teaches that the resurrection was a real, bodily event. Not a spiritual metaphor, not a vision experienced by grieving disciples, but the actual raising of a dead man in a transformed physical body (CCC 638-658). This is not a secondary point of Catholic teaching. Paul writes without qualification: "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17). The resurrection is the hinge on which everything else turns. Remove it and Christianity is not a diminished religion. It is no religion at all.

What the Church asks of someone hearing this for the first time is not immediate certainty. It asks honest engagement. The evidence is real. The questions it raises are serious. And the invitation has been the same for two thousand years: come and see.

7
For your life

Something shifts when you stop treating Jesus as a historical curiosity and start taking seriously the possibility that he was telling the truth. Not just about how to treat people, though he was right about that. About who God is. About what you are. About what death means and what comes after it.

If the resurrection happened, then human life has a weight to it that nothing else gives it. Forgiveness is real. The suffering you carry is not meaningless. The people you love are not headed toward oblivion. None of that comes from wishful thinking. It comes from a tomb that was empty on a Sunday morning two thousand years ago, and the question of what to make of that is one of the few questions that genuinely cannot be ignored forever.

What comes next

The Order of Christian Initiation of Adults

If any part of what you've read across these two modules has stayed with you, if a question opened up that you want to keep pulling on, the Catholic Church has a formal process for exactly that. It is called OCIA: the Order of Christian Initiation of Adults.

OCIA is not a membership drive. It is a guided conversation, usually meeting once a week over several months, where you can bring every question you have into a room with people who take both the faith and the questions seriously. You are not required to become Catholic at the end. Many people go through it simply to understand what the Church actually teaches before deciding what they believe.

Most parishes run OCIA starting in the fall, though many welcome people at any point in the year. You can walk into any Catholic parish and ask about it without any obligation whatsoever. If you want to keep going here first, Module 2 is waiting.

Some parishes may still refer to this process as RCIA. The name changed nationally in 2025 but the journey is identical under either name.
