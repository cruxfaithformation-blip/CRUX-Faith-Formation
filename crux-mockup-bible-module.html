Why Romans?

Paul had never been to Rome when he wrote this letter. He was planning to visit. He wanted to use the city as a launching point for a mission to Spain, and before he arrived, he wanted them to know exactly what he believed. So he wrote them a letter. What came out is the most systematic account of his theology in the entire New Testament.

Romans is not primarily a letter about moral behavior. It is a letter about one question: How can a human being be right with God? Paul's answer is not what most people expect.

2
The Righteousness of God (1:16–17)

Romans 1:17 is the hinge on which the entire letter turns. Paul quotes the prophet Habakkuk: "The righteous shall live by faith." But the phrase that matters most is what comes right before it: "the righteousness of God is revealed."

Romans 1:17

"For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, 'The righteous shall live by faith.'"

Word Study δικαιοσύνη dikaiosynē G1343
righteousness justice right standing covenant faithfulness

Most English translations say "righteousness," but the Greek means something more specific: the condition of being in right standing, of having your status declared correct. Think of it less as a personality trait and more as a legal verdict. In a Jewish context, it is also tied to covenant faithfulness, both God's faithfulness to his promises and the human response that God accepts. The Catholic tradition insists this righteousness is not merely declared but actually given, communicated through grace. That distinction, imputed vs. infused, was the fault line of the Reformation.

When Paul says "the righteousness of God is revealed," he is saying that in the Gospel, God's covenant faithfulness becomes visible. God is shown to be the kind of God who keeps his promises, even when his people have failed to keep theirs. That is not first a moral claim. It is a claim about what kind of God exists.

Commentary

The Catholic tradition reads Romans 1:17 as a declaration that God's saving justice, his dikaiosynē, is both given and transformative. The Council of Trent (Session VI) engaged Paul's language directly, insisting that justification involves an interior renewal, not merely a legal declaration. That is where Catholic interpretation parts ways with much of Protestant exegesis, which tends to read "righteousness" as forensic acquittal alone.

Scott Hahn notes that the phrase "from faith for faith" (ek pisteōs eis pistin) echoes the structure of the covenant: God's faithfulness calls forth human faithfulness in response. The righteousness revealed in the Gospel is not the standard God holds us to. It is the gift God gives to those who cannot meet that standard.

Church Fathers

For he did not say, "the righteousness of God is revealed," but "is being revealed," showing that the revelation is still in progress, and that this means not a single past event but a continuing disclosure of God's fidelity to his people.

St. John Chrysostom — Homilies on Romans, Homily II (c. AD 390)

Lectionary Connection
Romans 1:16–17 appears in the Roman Rite on the 10th Sunday in Ordinary Time (Year B). The next time this passage is proclaimed at Mass, you will have already studied it. That changes how you hear it.

Habakkuk 2:4 — Why Quote the Old Testament Here?

Paul quotes "The righteous shall live by faith" from the prophet Habakkuk, who wrote during the Babylonian exile, when Israel had been handed over to a pagan empire and God's faithfulness seemed in serious doubt. Paul quotes this deliberately. He is saying the Gospel is the answer to the question Habakkuk was asking. The same God who was faithful then is faithful now, and the proof is the resurrection.

Reflect

Augustine said that when he read Romans, he felt "a light of certainty flood his heart." Have you ever read something that changed how you understood everything else? A book, a passage, a single sentence? What was it, and what shifted?

3
All Have Sinned (3:9–20)

Romans 3:9–20 is the darkest passage in Paul's letters. He closes every exit: no one is righteous, no one understands, no one seeks God. The law itself is not the solution. It is, says Paul, the indictment.
